Action Contextualization: Adaptive Task Planning
and Action Tuning using Large Language Models
Sthithpragya Gupta1,†, Kunpeng Yao1,†,∗, Member, IEEE, Lo¨ıc Niederhauser1, and Aude Billard 1, Fellow, IEEE
Abstract—Large Language Models (LLMs) present a promising
frontier in robotic task planning by leveraging extensive human
knowledge. Nevertheless, the current literature often overlooks
the critical aspects of robots’ adaptability and error correction.
This work aims to overcome this limitation by enabling robots to
modify their motions and select the most suitable task plans based
on the context. We introduce a novel framework to achieve action
contextualization, aimed at tailoring robot actions to the context
of specific tasks, thereby enhancing adaptability through applying
LLM-derived contextual insights. Our framework integrates
motion metrics that evaluate robot performances for each motion
to resolve redundancy in planning. Moreover, it supports online
feedback between the robot and the LLM, enabling immediate
modifications to the task plans and corrections of errors. An
overall success rate of 81.25% has been achieved through
extensive experimental validation. Finally, when integrated with
dynamical system (DS)-based robot controllers, the robotic
arm-hand system demonstrates its proficiency in autonomously
executing LLM-generated motion plans for sequential table-
clearing tasks, rectifying errors without human intervention,
and showcasing robustness against external disturbances. Our
proposed framework also features the potential to be integrated
with modular control approaches, significantly enhancing robots’
adaptability and autonomy in performing sequential tasks in the
real world.
Index Terms—Task and motion planning, large language model,
action grounding, robot action adaptation.
I. I NTRODUCTION
Task and Motion Planning (TAMP) in robotics aims to devise
motion sequences that guide a robot toward a predetermined
goal, integrating task planning with motion planning. Effective
TAMP requires environmental information to ensure coherent,
achievable, and constraint-compliant plans. Traditional task
planning relies on comprehensive environmental descriptions
and substantial computational resources, often using languages
like PDDL (Planning Domain Definition Language) or STL
(Signal Temporal Logic). Such model-based approaches often
require manual input, such as domain files and problem files for
PDDL or signal descriptions and temporal logic formulas for
STL, restricting the autonomy of the robot and the feasibility
of solutions when facing unknown environments.

Large Language Models (LLMs) have recently shown
promise in various robotic tasks, offering a new avenue for
TAMP solutions. The recent advancement of LLMs offers a
promising solution for tackling TAMP problems in robotics [1,
2, 3, 4]. LLMs can leverage extensive human knowledge to
generate robot task plans [5, 6] or directly program robots [7],
alleviating the need for explicit manual modeling of detailed
task information. However, LLMs can produce unreliable
outputs, particularly in ensuring the feasibility on real robots,
due to their black-box nature. This gap between conceptual
plans and physical execution can hinder task feedback inte-
gration, leading to failures. Hence, LLM-based approaches
for robots are often limited to simple pick-and-place tasks in
simulations, struggling with long-horizon manipulations, in
particular, the ones involving physical interactions. To address
these challenges, LLMs must generate logical task plans that are
both feasible for robots and adaptive to task contexts, satisfying
explicit and implicit constraints. We refer to this capability
as action contextualization . For this purpose, we present a
framework that allows for tuning parameters of robotic actions
according to the inferred task context. The framework also
evaluates motion viability through simulations and defined
metrics, resolving redundancies in planning.
Our main contributions are threefold:
• First, we elaborate on a nuanced approach for tuning action
parameters, such as velocity and orientation, enhancing
the adaptability of motions to the specific demands of
each step of the task;
• Second, we leverage feedback for motion tuning and
strategic task replanning, and employ a motion evaluation
scoring system to select the optimal plan;
• Third, we integrate LLM-based planning with an online
collision detection approach and DS-based robot con-
trollers, significantly enhancing robots’ robustness and
adaptability in the real world.
II. B ACKGROUND
A. Task and Motion Planning
Traditional methods for TAMP often rely on hierarchical
planning, integrating high-level task planning with low-level
motion planning [8]. [9] introduced multi-step planning as a
solution for TAMP in robotics, demonstrating its effectiveness
with a dual-armed robot handling a complex object. [10]
presented the Task Constructor framework in MoveIt! [11], en-
hancing the capabilities of robotic manipulation by allowing the
definition and planning of motions consisting of multiple sub-
tasks. While symbolic knowledge is crucial in task planning,

motion planning often involves continuous models of robot
trajectories or behaviors, presenting a significant challenge
in integrating symbolic and continuous paradigms. Sampling-
based methods and trajectory optimization have been used to
address this issue by turning task planning into a sampling
process within a probabilistic search space. However, this
approach may not always be effective in finding solutions
during complex task-level searches [12]. [13] proposed TMP-
RL, a framework integrating TAMP and reinforcement learning
(RL) to enhance robustness in planning across dynamic and
uncertain domains, thereby improving adaptability to previously
unseen domain dynamics. [14] developed a system with
a hierarchical planner that solves TAMP problems while
optimizing for parallelization. This system uses action models
based on a constraint formulation, allowing asynchronous
parallel execution of plans on dual-arm robots. However,
these traditional TAMP approaches often exhibit adaptability,
generalization, and computational efficiency limitations. This
is mainly due to their reliance on predefined models and
scenarios, which limits their ability to adapt to new or
complex environments, and the high computational demands
of planning in detailed, dynamic spaces. Moreover, many of
these approaches do not guarantee completeness, potentially
overlooking infeasible problems. We address this issue by
defining a set of actions and their desired features based on
robots’ capabilities without requiring explicit environmental
modeling. This allows the framework to be generalized to
similar robots, novel tasks, and unknown environments.
B. LLMs for TAMP in Robotic Tasks
With their ability to generalize and reason about various
domains, LLMs have demonstrated potential as task-agnostic
planners [15, 16], showcasing TAMP capabilities in various
robotic tasks, such as pick-and-place [3] and object rearrange-
ment [17]. Efforts to improve the effectiveness of LLMs include
incorporating human feedback to align better planning with
user intent [18]. In [19], the applicability of LLMs on robots
for tidying up a room, using commonsense knowledge and
a limited action set, was investigated. Incorporating domain
knowledge, LLMs have also been used as generalized planners
in PDDL domains [8, 20]. Moreover, to enhance LLM-based
robotic task planning performance, [6] proposed a validation
framework through iterative self-refinement to improve task
success rates. [21] proposed DROC, handling various language
feedbacks and retrieving past experiences, but relies heavily on
human corrections, which can be subjective, time-consuming,
and inconsistent. Our framework adapts by extracting task-
specific contextual information and tuning/replanning robotic
actions in simulation. More recently, [4] proposed RobotGPT to
improve task success by leveraging ChatGPT’s problem-solving
insights within a stable framework, addressing limitations, such
as unpredictability and safety concerns. Despite the promising
advancements, there remains a considerable gap in fully inte-
grating these advanced planning capabilities with real robotic
applications. Challenges persist in ensuring these approaches’
safety, interpretability, and real-world applicability. In this paper,
we demonstrate that this gap can be bridged by employing
dynamical system-based adaptive robot controllers for low-
level motion control, ensuring adaptability and robustness while
executing the high-level task plan.
C. Action Grounding
A key aspect of language-aided robotic task execution is
grounding language commands into executable robot actions
[22]. Operator grounding, i.e., connecting abstract, language-
based task instructions to their practical execution by robots,
serving as a subset of the broader symbolic grounding problem,
establishes the link between the abstract language-based
task instructions and their practical execution on the real
robots. Specific models such as [23, 24, 25] can determine
connections between elements of the language instructions and
the semantic entities in the environment. However, they often
do not include the underlying relational knowledge, which
refers to understanding how different concepts, objects, or
entities relate to each other within a context in their reasoning
process, potentially limiting the depth of comprehension and
interaction with the environment. The specific context of a task
significantly influences the physical actions undertaken by the
actual robots [1]. Crucially, it’s essential to effectively link the
LLM’s abstract instructions to the robot’s concrete actions to
ensure successful task completion. Contemporary works have
started leveraging LLMs to achieve action grounding by guiding
the agent’s interaction with the environment [26]. For example,
low-level pre-trained skills combined with LLMs can facilitate
knowledge acquisition and task execution [2]. [27] proposed
an approach to translate abstract instruction into formal logic
that the robots can interpret. In contrast, [28] assessed the
geometric feasibility during the robot’s action selection. [5,
29] utilized prompting to extract the context established by
the task and identify the admissible actions for the robot to
execute. Action grounding with LLMs still faces challenges
such as satisfying real-world physical constraints, ensuring the
feasibility for robots, and bridging the gap between planned
and actual outcomes. These issues highlight the difficulty in
translating abstract LLM instructions into tangible robotic
actions, indicating the need for improved methodologies. We
address these challenges by implementing a prior validation,
including motion tuning and task replanning to verify constraint
satisfaction and action feasibility. We also employ online
collision detection and DS-based robot controllers to ensure
adaptive and robust execution.

Task plan The first step towards real-world task execution
starts with preparing a plan consisting of a sequence of actions
for the robot. This plan, formulated as a Python list of the
actions’ symbolic expressions (listed in Table I), is hereby
referred to as the task_plan.
The LLM is prompted to generate this list, only utilizing
the actions in the repertoire (Table I) to ensure the plan does
not exceed the robot’s movement ability.
Each action is parameterized by a combination of vari-

and grasp, and (2) quantitative parameters, which are nu-
meric values, such as speed, orientation, and obsta-

action sequences in the task_plan, whereas the quantitative
parameters regulate the robot behavior (see Table II).
The LLM initializes the task_planwith an initial esti-
mation of actions and their associated parameters and tunes
them over subsequent iterations. This is further explained in
Sec. III-B.

B. Adaptation
Once the task_planand evaluation_planare initial-
ized, the framework starts to iteratively tune the action param-
eters during the simulated task execution. This is carried out
using the LLM, in accordance with the task context. Finally, the
most suited task_planand associated evaluation_plan
are selected for the robot to perform the task (see Fig. 1).
1) Iterative motion adaptation
The initialized task_plan undergoes simulation-based
validation, where the evaluation_plandetermines the
expected/ideal behavior and provides automated feedback,
which includes:
• Failed step of the task_plan, i.e., the unsuccessful
action;
• Reason of failure: determined by the failed check-in
evaluation_plan;
• A motion score associated with each action executed until
failure (see Sec. III-B2).
The feedback is stored as performance_log.
In addition, the parameters of both successful and un-
successful actions (and the corresponding objects being ma-
nipulated) are also logged in parameter_history. The
feedback informs regarding the failed action, whereas the log of
successful and failed parameters for the action is used to inform
the tuning process. Post tuning, the resulting task_plan
undergoes validation anew. The resultant of the execution is
used to update the performance_logand append to the
parameter_history. This process constitutes one cycle of
the retuneprocess. The retuneprocess continues until the
framework converges to a tuned task_plan, or a certain
amount of iteration has been conducted.
If the tuned task_planremains unsuccessful, the re-
plan procedure prompts the LLM to assess feedback from
the evaluation_planand revise the action sequence in the
task plan. During this procedure, adjustments may be made
to various aspects of the task_plan, including the object-
interaction sequence (e.g., interacting first with obj2instead
of obj1); action parameters; the type of actions (e.g., using
dropinstead of throw).
Once the task_planpasses simulation-based validation,
it will be applied to guide the real robotic system to perform
tasks in the real world.
2) Motion evaluation
Multiple motions could be feasible to achieve the desired
effect(s) at a step of the plan due to redundancy. For example,
the robotic arm can reach a target object via multiple feasible
trajectories and grasp the object from different directions, even
using different hand poses.
To resolve the redundancy in generated motions, we evaluate
sets of feasible motions across multiple independently generated
plans and use the one having the highest quality score, Q, which
considers both internal and external metrics.
The internal quality metric MI evaluates the quality of
robot configuration during the motion. The dexterity index
[30], defined as µmax/µmin with µmax and µmin being the
largest and smallest singular values of the manipulator Jacobian
matrix J, respectively. It indicates the “uniformity” of the
manipulability ellipsoid [31]. We use the inverse of the original
dexterity index, δ = µmin/µmax, so that its value is normalized
in (0, 1]. The “ideal” configuration has a value δ = 1, indicating
an isotropic configuration [32], where the end-effector can
freely move in all directions in the space. The index value
approaches 0 as the robot moves towards a singularity, i.e., a
configuration with impaired motion ability in some directions,
hence being unable to execute subsequent motions. We keep
track of the metric values during the entire motion trajectory,
and the smallest value recorded is assigned as the internal
score of the motion: MI = min{δ}.
The external quality metric ME evaluates the safety of
the robotic system, measured by its minimal distance to
the environment during the motion, ME = min {di}/ ¯d,
i = 1, . . . , NL, where di is the minimal distance of the ith link
to its environment, measured by the neural distance function; ¯d
is the maximum distance from any robot link to the workspace’s
border (the region of interest). NL is the total number of links in

C. Execution
Finally, after Planning (Sec. III-A) and Tuning (Sec. III-B),
the most effective plan for accomplishing the specified task
is identified. To ensure real-world execution efficacy, the
framework must be robust to failure during robot execution
and incorporate additional feedback from a human expert.
The simulated success of the finalized task_planmay
not necessarily carry on to the real world. It is required
to plan for failure alleviation. When faced with failure,
the replan procedure is promptly called. Contrary to the
retune procedure implemented during the tuning process
in Sec. III-B, during real-world execution, the replanpro-
cedure leverages feedback from the evaluation_planto
alter the task_plan from the current step. This allows
the agent to correct the failure induced during the previous
unsuccessful action before proceeding with the alteration and
(or) execution of the remainder of the steps. For instance,
a failed pick(apple) would require the agent first to
execute approach(apple)before retrying pick(apple).
In addition to using the feedback from evaluation_plan,
both replan and retune are capable of using additional
external feedback during execution, such as from a human
expert or new sensory information.
IV. E XPERIMENTS
In this section, we conduct experiments to assess our
proposed approaches. We first describe the experimental task
and system specification in Sec. IV-A. In Sec. IV-B, we evaluate
the framework’s capability for tuning parameters for a sequence
of actions. Sec. IV-C highlights the framework’s context-aware
adjustment ability by demonstrating how the framework selects
appropriate actions (and parameters) according to the object and
subsequent manipulation goals by leveraging the prior human
knowledge encapsulated in the LLM. Sec. IV-D extends this
evaluation to tuning parameters across an entire task plan,
demonstrating the LLM’s effectiveness in task planning and
action selection. In Sec. IV-E, we test different LLM models
to perform the same task. Next, in Sec. IV-F, we test the
robustness of the framework to instances of randomization in
the environment. Finally, in Sec. IV-G, we proceed with the
real-world robotic execution of the best task plan generated by
the framework.

A. Experimental Setup
1) Task description
We consider a table-cleaning task to evaluate our framework:
a robotic arm-hand system is tasked to efficiently clean the
surface of a messy dining table with various objects placed on
it, including a whole apple, a half-eaten apple, a full champagne
glass, an empty champagne glass, and five paper balls (see
Fig. 3). Next to the table is a trash can to throw residual food
and a sink to wash dishes. The corresponding environment in
simulation is presented in Fig. 4, where the only difference is
we simulate two paper balls instead of all five. In such a long-
horizon sequential task, the robot must (1) generate a sequence
of tackling the cluster of objects and (2) automatically choose
the correct motion and set a goal for each object, respectively.
In addition to logically performing a sequence of motion, the
robot needs to closely interact with the objects, such as grasping
and polishing. Moreover, such motions must be performed in
a crowded environment, increasing the task difficulty.
2) System specification
We used a robotic setup with a 7-DoF KUKA iiwa arm
equipped with a 16-DoF Allegro hand as the end-effector. This
setup is used in both simulations and real robotic experiments.
We used MuJoCo 1 as a physics engine for simulation. We use
GPT-4-1106-previewas our LLM engine in this paper.
For comparison between different LLM models in Sec. IV-E,

V. D ISCUSSION
A. LLM for Robotic TAMP
Our experiments showed that as part of the framework, pro-
viding action descriptions (Table I) to the LLM, in conjunction
with the task and scene descriptions, enables it to establish
correspondence between the action’s tunable parameters and
success metrics established by the task (and context in the
scene). We demonstrated parameter tuning and motion scoring
for single-object manipulation tasks with different objects
in Sec. IV-B. The framework can leverage the human-like
intelligence and common sense embedded in the LLM, when
tuning action parameters throughout multiple retune and
replanprocedures (Fig. 6).

size of the context that can be accommodated by the LLM
model for long-horizon planning and (or) tuning. For instance,
the Meta-Llama-3-70B model, with a (currently) limited context

When performing robotic manipulation in a complex human
environment such as a kitchen, it is often challenging to
quantify the optimality of a planned strategy. This complexity
can arise due to various obstacles, objects (interactive and
non-interactive), and the implicit physical constraints they
impose. The ability to leverage contextual information about
the task (and environment) from natural language and utilize
common sense allows the formulated strategies to avoid failing
such constraints. For instance, when performing pick and
placeactions with the full glass, it is crucial to maintain the
orientation of the glass to avoid spillage of its contents. While
performing similar actions on the half-eaten apple, manually
defining an objective function that maximizes execution speed
may yield ideal results. However, the same objective function
may yield undesirable results with the filled glass on account
of not prioritizing object orientation over execution speed.
Such task contextualization enables motion adaptation during
planning (Fig. 6, (a) and (b)) as well as parameter tuning
(Fig. 6, (c) and (d)) resulting in a robust task execution.
This is also observed in Sec. IV-D, where a multi-object
manipulation task is specified. For example, in Fig. 7, the LLM
can reason about its persistent failure to pickthe eaten apple
and formulates a new plan first to put away the filled glass. This
is interesting as no explicit spatial information about the objects
or the robot’s whereabouts is provided within the framework.
Using the context, automated feedback from the framework,
and following it up with commonsense reasoning, the LLM
successfully plans and tunes the task_plan. Generating,
simulating, and scoring multiple such plans allows us to pick
the highest-scoring plan for robotic execution (Fig. 8).
Essentially, the action tuning and replanning ability can be
considered parameter fitting using LLM. The benefit lies in
leveraging human life experience (i.e., prior knowledge) when
planning motions for everyday tasks that heavily rely on human
life experience and have also been intensively demonstrated
by humans. In comparison to model-based approaches such
as optimal control or constrained optimization, LLM-based
planning demonstrated the ability to have a suitable initial
guess of the motion characteristics without explicit prior
knowledge provided by humans, making it possible to embed
context-sensitive task parameters, achieving context-aware
TAMP autonomously. In contrast to learning-based approaches,
LLM excels in computational efficiency, as it can achieve a
satisfactory performance within only a few steps, with no need
for collecting large amounts of training data. These features
of LLM can be especially beneficial in dealing with high-
dimensionality problems, such as planning long sequential
motions and achieving human-like behavior in everyday life.

our experiments, LLM also failed in certain cases and showed

b) Error detection and resolution
The aforementioned instances demonstrate how the LLM
is susceptible to erroneous planning. A method to detect and
remove such planning inconsistencies is using post hoc verifica-
tion: checking the status of each motion and retuning/replanning
the motions in simulation before executing on a real robotic
system, e.g., using the proposed evaluation_plan.
First, plans that violate the logic will be detected due to
a mismatch between observations and desired values from
self-placed checks. Second, the proposed framework conducts
simulation-based validation to assess the feasibility of the
robotic system executing the planned actions. Even if an
erroneous plan manages to pass the placed evaluation checks,
it will fail in the simulation. Lastly, the generated plan has a
Python format, and code compilation prior to execution serves
as a tertiary check to identify indexing errors or ambiguous
correspondence between actions and checks. When a plan is
flagged, the LLM is prompted to retune, replan, or correct
syntax. By generating multiple independent plans following
the same pipeline and scoring them independently, the incorrect
or sub-optimal plans get dropped out. The framework detects
and (or) resolves such instances as follows:
• The plan contains repeated actions: Such plans are flagged

achieve success when repeating an action with the same
parameters but via different starting conditions;
• Semantically disoriented actions: When an action is used
incorrectly (e.g., using place instead of drop), the plan once
again gets flagged either by the self-placed action-specific
predicate checks or during simulation due to physical
constraints. This happens because the behavior of the
robot controller responsible for executing the specified
action does not match up with the expected outcome
(either in simulation or during self-evaluation);
• Performing redundant actions: At times, the plan included
redundant actions, such as picking an object from the
table only to place it back on the table. Such actions are
non-detrimental to the task’s success or simply get flagged
during simulation due to code equivalence.
c) Excessive retuning is risky
The key objective of motion adaptation ( retune) in our
framework is to achieve task success and improve motion
quality, not necessarily achieve optimality, and must be used
as such. The parameter tuning step aims to let LLM robustly
assess the feasibility of the previously generated task_plan.
While we score these motions, they serve more as hindsight to
LLM. As stated before, retunelacks an objective function
(or even a metric) to quantify the task performance to arrive
at a set of globally optimum parameters. Forcing retune
post achieving success did not necessarily exhibit a positive
correlation with the accompanying motion scores (see Fig. 5).
This might indicate a limitation in LLM: although the physical
meaning of each metric is described, the LLM may struggle
to adjust the robot system without a detailed model. This
may imply a disconnect between the LLM’s abstract and
high-level task understanding and the specific, real-world
requirements of robotic operations, hence highlighting the
importance of integrating detailed robotic models with LLMs
for more challenging action tuning. Thus, parameter tuning
should be used only to achieve task success.
d) The performance fluctuation in LLM
to use LLM for TAMP, it is crucial to ensure stable
performance by the LLM. However, we have noticed that
the response quality and response time of LLM can vary
greatly depending on the time of the day. The best performance
is typically achieved during 0800 − 1600 CET; degraded
performance is outside this time window. While not encountered
recently, lazy planning is also observed in LLM, especially for
multi-object manipulation tasks. When this occurs, the LLM
usually prescribes a pick-placeroutine for a few objects and
then comments in task_planto carry on similarly, without
providing relevant actions and parameters. The retuning and
replanning steps in our framework can only help prevent such
plans from being used to instruct the real robot but cannot
eliminate this issue.
C. Integrating LLM into Real Robotic System
The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed
framework is able to leverage LLMs into creating context-aware
motions, enhancing the robot’s adaptability to underlying task
constraints and requirements. The main hurdle when integrating
LLMs with actual robotic systems is the real-world interaction,
mainly due to the LLM’s inability to adjust robot movements
in real-time, such as responding to disturbances or avoiding
collision in a changing environment. Therefore, it’s imperative
for the controller to equip the robot with robust and adaptive
motions while avoiding both self-collision and collisions with
the external environment in real time. We first tested this in
simulation by setting up new scenarios for task execution.
Between the original and the first new scenario (Figs. 10 (a)
and (b)), adding randomness to the positions of objects in
the scene allowed us to test the adaptability of the controllers
to the action-parameter combinations. In the second setting
(Fig. 10(c)), adding a lamp as an additional obstacle in the
workspace allowed us to test collision avoidance.
In our real-world tests, the DS-based controller significantly
improved the robot’s ability to adjust and maintain stable perfor-
mance, even under deliberate human interference. Additionally,
the neural distance function facilitated immediate collision
detection by inferring the robot’s collision status in real time.
This avoids the voxelization of the real world [35], which can
hardly be applied to deal with a dynamic environment in real
time. This highlights the essential role of an adaptive controller
and collision detection algorithms in ensuring successful task

VI. C ONCLUSION
In this paper, we presented an LLM-based framework
for robotic TAMP that incorporates action contextualization,
adapting robot motion to the task context using insights
from human experience embedded in the LLM. Our approach
uniquely allows for online adjustment and replanning based
on simulated outcomes, mitigated by motion scores, to address
the LLM’s lack of awareness of robotic models and dynamics,
ensuring more appropriate motion profiles for actual robots. The
integration of these elements offers a robust solution bridging
task planning and robotic execution. Notably, the success of our
real robotic experiments is largely attributed to the dynamical
system-based robot controller and collision avoidance ability,
which facilitates adaptive and reactive robot behavior in real
time. The proposed framework can easily be extended to
other real-world manipulation tasks, where context is crucial
during planning and execution. The framework facilitates a
straightforward integration with modular control strategies, such
as Dynamic Movement Primitives. Essential to this integration
is an appropriate parameterization of motion elements, which
should capture the contextual details of the task, thus leveraging
the potential benefits of LLM. Furthermore, by defining new
actions and adding their descriptions to the existing stack, it
is also possible to use user-defined robotic behavior during
task execution. Similarly, defining new checks and adding their
descriptions enables verification of the task execution. These
aspects collectively contribute to an easy-to-program robotic
agent for real-world task execution. Despite the successes, the
LLM’s black-box nature presents inherent limitations and risks,
warranting caution. In our future work, we plan to explore
more complex interactions with the environment, validating our
approach in unknown environments, including scenarios such
as exploration with mobile manipulators or humanoid robots.
Additionally, we will explore selective score prioritization for
motion refinement and the development of a local skill model
within the LLM for enhanced task-specific learning. Lastly,
we aim to generalize the formulation of our action sets to
facilitate seamless integration with modular control approaches,
significantly expanding the applicability of our framework.

drop(location: str, speed: float, obstacle_clearance: float) − > None: # Goes to ‘location’ and drops the grasped object

LLM-based Robot Task Planning with Exceptional Handling for
General Purpose Service Robots
Ruoyu Wang∗ 1, Zhipeng Yang∗ 1, Zinan Zhao 1, Xinyan Tong 1, Zhi Hong 1, Kun Qian † 1
1. School of Automation, Southeast University, Nanjing, 210096
E-mail: kqian@seu.edu.cn
Abstract: The development of a general purpose service robot for daily life necessitates the robot’s ability to deploy a myriad
of fundamental behaviors judiciously. Recent advancements in training Large Language Models (LLMs) can be used to generate
action sequences directly, given an instruction in natural language with no additional domain information. However, while the
outputs of LLMs are semantically correct, the generated task plans may not accurately map to acceptable actions and might
encompass various linguistic ambiguities. LLM hallucinations pose another challenge for robot task planning, which results in
content that is inconsistent with real-world facts or user inputs. In this paper, we propose a task planning method based on a
constrained LLM prompt scheme, which can generate an executable action sequence from a command. An exceptional handling
module is further proposed to deal with LLM hallucinations problem. This module can ensure the LLM-generated results are
admissible in the current environment. We evaluate our method on the commands generated by the RoboCup@Home Command
Generator, observing that the robot demonstrates exceptional performance in both comprehending instructions and executing
tasks.
Key Words:Task Planning, General Purpose Service Robots, Large Language Model
1 Introduction
The development of a general purpose service robot for
daily life has long been an aspiration. A primary challenge
in this task lies in enabling robots to comprehend the com-
mand in natural language and subsequently execute corre-
sponding actions in a coherent sequence. This necessitates
the robot’s ability to deploy a series of fundamental behav-
iors judiciously, sorting and executing these behaviors under
the overall task requirements. For instance, when presented
with the command, Could you find a female person in the
living room and answer her question? the robot should in-
fer a sequence of actions: first, navigate to the living room,
then identify a female individual, and finally, respond to her
question.
Traditionally, these challenges have been approached
from the perspectives of lexical analysis[16, 17]. However,
high-level task planning often demands the definition of ex-
tensive domain knowledge related to the robot’s operating
environment and understanding of semantic knowledge. Au-
toregressive large language models (LLMs) trained on exten-
sive corpora precisely address these requirements. Recent
advancements in training Large Language Models (LLMs)
have empowered systems to generate intricate texts based
on prompts, answer questions, and engage in conversations
spanning a wide range of topics. This capability has been ap-
plied to generate acceptable action plans within the context
of robotic task planning[2, 14], either by scoring the next
steps or directly generating new steps.
However, while the outputs of LLMs are semantically cor-
rect, the generated task plans may not accurately map to
acceptable actions and might encompass various linguistic
ambiguities [21]. Prior works also introduce LLM halluci-

2 Related Work
Various prior works have looked into using language
as a space for planning. In the beginning, a series of
them[16, 17] applied lexical analysis to parse language in-
structions and resolve linguistic ambiguities, requiring many
hand-designed rules to adapt to complex tasks and envi-
ronments. Recently, since the remarkable ability large lan-
guage models has shown in multitask generalization, many
task planning approaches leverage pre-trained autoregressive
LLMs to decompose abstract and high-level instructions into
a sequence of low-level steps.
Brown et al.[7] showed that zero-shot or few-shot prompt-
ing could be highly effective for transfer learning. Further-
more, chain-of-thought prompting[25] proved that even sim-
ple prompting modifications can profoundly boost LLMs’
performance. Li et al.[14] incorporated environment con-
text and predicted actions in VirtualHome[19] by fine-tuning
GPT-2. In contrast, Huang et al.[10] investigated exist-
ing knowledge in LLMs (GPT-3[7] and Codex[8]) to gen-
erate action plans for embodied agents, without any addi-
tional training. SayCan[2] applied a learned value function
to evaluate each candidate action generated by LLM, which
serves as a proxy for affordance. However, It may scale
inadequately to dynamic environments with combinatorial
action spaces. Inner Monologue[11] introduced grounded
feedback from the environment to the LLM when generat-
ing each step in the plan and highly improved high-level in-
struction completion. PROGPROMPT[21] proposed a pro-
grammatic LLM prompt structure containing program-like
specifications of the available actions and objects in an en-
vironment, which enables LLM to generate an entire, exe-
cutable plan program directly. Obinata et al.[18] first uti-
lized LLM(GPT-3[7]) to perform General Purpose Service
Robot (GPSR) tasks in robocup@home, getting high scores
in the competition. However, it may not be robust in han-
dling LLM hallucinationss [9, 13] error. Based on this, We
have incorporated exception handling and made compensa-
tions for weaknesses in our prompt design, resulting in a
higher task completion rate.

3.2 Generating State Machines with LLM
We propose a task planning method fprompt(·) based on
a constrained LLM prompt scheme, which can generate an
executable actions sequence from a command in natural lan-
guage. An exceptional handling module is further proposed
to deal with LLM hallucinationss problem [9, 13]. With our
proposed task planning method, the LLM can generate an
executable action sequence in the form of a Pythonic list,
which can be used to construct the SMACH [6] state ma-
chine.
3.2.1 Constrained LLM Prompt
Specifically, we define eight primitive actions:“move to”,
“look for obj”, “look for person”, “follow”, “grasp”, “pass
to”, “speak”, and “answer”, which can implement all com-
mands generated by RoboCup@Home Command Genera-
tor [1]. Firstly, we provide these primitive actions to the
LLM as Pythonic import statements, to ensure the gener-
ated action sequences are executable. For example, in the
task Give me a cola , the LLM may generate the first action
“[find, cola]”. However, the robot might not define a prim-
itive action to find or have already had a similar primitive
action such as“look for obj”. As shown in Fig. 1, we explic-
itly import executable primitive actions as Pythonic function
structures. These encourage the LLM to constrain the task
planning result to executable primitive actions that are pre-
defined. And the available objects in the environment can be
used to be the parameters of Pythonic action functions.
To facilitate the LLM has more precise comprehension of
task nature and requirements, we provide a few parsing ex-
amples to LLM for few-shot learning. These parsing exam-
ples are fully executable and show the LLM how to parse a
given command using the predefined primitive actions and
complete tasks in a subjectively reasonable manner. Due
to token length limitations, we provide 23 parsing exam-
ples, covering a broad spectrum of commands generated
by RoboCup@Home Command Generator[1]. For complex
commands prone to misunderstanding, we provide multiple
examples to enable the LLM to learn the task planning exam-
ple accurately. For example, in the task Meet Jennifer at the
sink, follow her, and take her back, it is hard for the LLM to
understand take her back and parse into the form “[actions,
parameters]”. Thus we interpret “back” as “initial location”
and the command Meet Jennifer at the sink, follow her, and
take her back is represented as “[[ move to, sink], [look for
person, Jennifer], [ follow, Jennifer], [ speak, Please follow
me!], [move to, initial location]]”.
Subsequently, we add parsing requirements, as shown in
Fig. 1. In this way, we can guide the LLM in responding
in the same format as the provided examples and help LLM
better clarify commands that are easy to confuse. Then we
add the current parsing task and input these sentences to the
LLM. Due to the few-shot learning and explicit parsing re-
quirements, the LLM finally generates an executable action
sequence in the form of a Pythonic list, which can be used to
construct the SMACH [6] state machine.
3.2.2 Exceptional Handling
We also employ an exceptional handling module to ensure
the LLM-generated results are admissible in the current envi-
ronment. Prior works introduce LLM hallucinationss [9, 13],
resulting in content that is inconsistent with real-world facts
or user inputs. For example, given a command Give me a
cola with the above prompt scheme, the task planning re-
sult could be “[ look for obj(cola)]” for faithfulness halluci-
nations which refers to the divergence of generated content
from user instructions, and “[find, cola]” for factuality hallu-
cinations which refers to factual inconsistency or fabrication
because we never define the primitive action find. To deal
with LLM hallucinations problem, we add new prompt in-
structions when we detect that the answer deviates from the
specified format or the parsed function list contains actions
beyond the provided primitive actions. We add a new prompt
Please note the format of the answer! and send a request to
LLM again when the answer deviates from the specified for-
mat. If the parsed action list contains actions beyond the pro-
vided primitive actions, a prompt is added Please note that
scheduled subtasks need to be used to complete task plan-
ning. The robot will determine that the command cannot be
parsed if an exception occurs five consecutive times.
3.3 Implementation of Primitive Actions
In this section, we delineate the defined primitive actions
and furnish their specific details:
1) “look for person” : Node A is the main node
of “look for person” function, which encompasses three
sub-states: EXPLORE ROOM, FACE TO PERSON, and
MOVE TO PERSON, designed for searching specific indi-
viduals in the environment. The functions of each state are
as follows:
• EXPLORE ROOM: Involves room exploration, human
detection, and identification of gesture characteristics.
• FACE TO PERSON: Directs the robot to face humans
and confirm their gender.
• MOVE TO PERSON: Guides the robot to move in
front of individuals meeting the specified criteria.
As shown in Fig 3, firstly, the robot rotates at a fixed point
and begins to execute node B. Depending on the given pa-
rameter, the robot will then detect the gesture characteristics
of people or facial characteristics. Finally, the detection re-
sults will be returned to Node A by the topic communica-
tion mechanism in ROS, for the robot executing subsequent
states.
2) “follow”: This action entails following the person in
front of the robot, implemented through MediaPipe [15] ges-
ture recognition and skeleton tracking. Specifically, we uti-
lize a RealSense depth camera for RGB images and depth
maps. Then we detect the human gesture from the video
frames, which determines subsequent states, including fol-
lowing, pausing, and termination. When the next state is
following, we locate the coordinates of the midpoint of the
human shoulders through skeleton tracking. By combining
the coordinates and depth maps, we can obtain the PD con-
trol quantity for robot motion.
3) Other Primitives: “move to” action aims to navigate
the robot to the target position. We define the coordinates of
different locations in the environment and use the movebase
package in ROS for path planning.
answer action aims to respond to questions from indi-
viduals. We employ the LLM to generate a response to a
question. The robot initiates the “answer” action, prompt-
ing a new round of Q&A. Parsing requirements are adjusted
to Please answer the following questions in English in no
more than 30 words. After obtaining the answer’s text in-
formation, the “speak” action is employed to respond to the
questioner. The Q&A module incorporates a mechanism to
determine the inability to answer after 5 consecutive failed
attempts.

and follow, (B)tasks requiring the robot to look for an object
and pass, (C)tasks requiring the robot to speak or answer
someone’s question. We assess the accuracy of each model
for each task category. The accuracy refers to the percent-
age of correctly parsed primitive functions with parameters

5 Conclusion and Future Work
In this paper, we propose a task planning method based
on a constrained LLM prompt scheme, which can generate
an executable action sequence from a command. An excep-
tional handling module is further proposed to deal with the
LLM hallucinations problem. LLM hallucinations could re-
sult in content that is inconsistent with real-world facts or
user inputs, including faithfulness hallucinations and factu-
ality hallucinations. We add new prompt instructions when
we detect that the answer deviates from the specified for-
mat or the parsed function list contains actions beyond the
provided primitive actions. This module can ensure the
LLM-generated results are admissible in the current envi-
ronment. We evaluate our method on commands generated
by the RoboCup@Home Command Generator[1], observing
that the robot demonstrates exceptional performance in both
comprehending instructions and executing tasks.
While we present a viable way to employ LLM for robot
task planning and deal with LLM hallucinations issues, it
has limits. First, for the instruction sets that are more
semantically rich and abstract, such as ALFRED[20] and
BEHA VIOR[22], our defined primitive actions may not be
executable due to the lack of specific individuals or ob-
jects as arguments. In future work, Visual Language Models
could be introduced to extract additional semantic informa-
tion from the environment, thus enhancing the robot’s gen-
eralization capabilities. Secondly, our exceptional handling
module can take effect after the occurrence of LLM hallu-
cinations issues, yet its capability remains limited, requiring
deeper research on designing more effective prompts to re-
duce the occurrence of illusion problems.

Combining Ontological Knowledge and Large Language Model f or
User-Friendly Service Robots
Haru Nakajima and Jun Miura
Department of Computer Science and Engineering
T oyohashi University of T echnology
Abstract— Lifestyle support through robotics is an increas-
ingly promising ﬁeld, with expectations for robots to take o ver
or assist with chores like ﬂoor cleaning, table setting and
clearing, and fetching items. The growth of AI, particularl y
foundation models, such as large language models (LLMs) and
visual language models (VLMs), is signiﬁcantly shaping thi s
sector . LLMs, by facilitating natural interactions and pro viding
vast general knowledge, are proving invaluable for robotic tasks.
This paper zeroes in on the beneﬁts of LLMs for ”bring-
me” tasks, where robots fetch speciﬁc items for users, often
based on vague instructions. Our previous efforts utilized an
ontology extended to handle environmental data to decipher
such vagueness, but faced limitations when unresolvable am bi-
guities required user intervention for clarity . Here, we en hance
our approach by integrating LLMs for providing additional
commonsense knowledge, pairing it with ontological data to
mitigate the issue of hallucinations and reduce the need for
user queries, thus improving system usability . W e present a
system that merges these knowledge bases and assess its efﬁc acy
on ”bring-me” tasks, aiming to provide a more seamless and
efﬁcient robotic assistance experience.
I. I N T RO D U CT IO N
Lifestyle support is one of the promising application areas
of robotics technologies, and many chore tasks are expected
to be replaced or supported by service robots. Possible chor es
include ﬂoor cleaning, setting and clearing tables, and ﬁnd ing
and bringing objects, among others, and various systems
have been developed that can interact with the users using
knowledge about the task and environment [1], [2], [3].
Along with the rapid advancement of AI technologies, the
expectations for such service robots are greatly rising. Th e
biggest recent advancement is undoubtedly generative AI,
especially those based on foundation models, such as large
language models (LLMs) [4], [5] and visual language models
(VLMs) [6].
Introducing LLMs into service robots is effective as they
not only enable natural dialog between robot and human
but also provide general knowledge that can be used for
robot control [7], [8], [9]. This paper focuses on the lat-
ter advantage of LLMs. W e deal with bring-me tasks, in
which a robot brings a user-speciﬁed object from a distant
place. Users’ commands can be ambiguous, where complete
information for robot action execution is partially missin g. In
our previous work [2], we used the ontology with extension
for handling facts in the environment to resolve ambiguitie s.
However, the recorded facts are limited and the robot always
asked the user for necessary detailed information when the
robot cannot resolve ambiguities completely by itself.
In this paper, we adopt a large language model (LLM) as
another source of knowledge. In addition to being able to
obtain commonsense-like knowledge from the LLM, com-
bining two knowledge sources can reduce the hallucination
problem by ﬁltering outputs from the LLM using the onto-
logical knowledge. This is effective to reduce the number
of inquiries to the user, thereby increasing the usability o f
the system. This paper develops a system that combines
the two knowledge sources and evaluates the performance
using bring-me tasks. One of the crucial issues in applying
LLMs to service robots is how to prevent hallicinations, as
we cannot completely address it even if we tune prompts as
much as possible. One approach is to introduce an explicit
mechanism for grounding outputs. Recent trends in retrieva l-
augmented text generation (RAG) [10], [11] seek similar
directions. Some work has proposed to use knowledge or
experiences to generate feasible robotic plans [12], [13], [14],
[15]. In this paper, we take this approach by combining onto-
logical knowledge with LLMs. The contribution of the paper
is twofold. First, we propose a new method of integrating
two knowledge sources. Second, we evaluate the method for
the “bring-me” task in terms of feasibility, efﬁciency, and
usability.
II. R E L AT E D WO RK
A. Service robots utilizing knowledge about the environmen t
and dialog
Home service robots must have knowledge about the
working environment and utilize it to automatically genera te
action plans. In “bring-me” tasks, for example, the type and
the location of the objects are crucial information. Severa l
studies have proposed the use of ontologies as basis of
knowledge representation [16], [17], [18]. W e have devel-
oped a robotic system that utilizes ontological knowledge
supported by verbal interaction to resolve ambiguities in
users’ commands [2]. When knowledge bases are incomplete
and uncertain, the robot may fail to retrieve correct or
complete information for task execution and needs to ask the
user for the missing information. Such a dialog-supported
strategy is effective but might degrade the usability of the
system by repeated inquiries.
Generating appropriate queries for increasing the possi-
bility of obtaining informative answers is a key to devel-
oping user-friendly service robots. Morohashi and Miura
[19] developed a method to generate queries that will yield
the most informative answer from the user. Pramanick et
al. [20] developed a method for ambiguity resolution by
identifying the type of ambiguity using visual scene analys is
and by generating appropriate inquiries for that type. Shin
et al. [3] proposed a framework enabling social robots to
generate questions that resolve uncertainties, taking int o
account knowledge categories, required cognitive process es,
and types of questions. Generating appropriate queries wil l
increase the usability of the system, but it would be nice if
we could reduce the number of queries further.
B. Large language models for robots
Large language models (LLMs) have been more and more
popular in robotics, in addition to many other domains, and
there are many attempts to make robotic plans using LLMs
[7], [8], [9], [13], [21], [22], [23], [24]. In these works,
one of the common issues is how to make LLM outputs be
consistent with (or grounded on) the current situation. Man y
of such works consider both the appropriateness and the fea-
sibility of plans to choose the best plan. Example command-
plan pairs, which are carefully-chosen or actually-held, a re
often input as prompts. They do not introduce a mechanism
of explicitly ﬁltering out ungrounded information.
Ocker et al. [23] explored the use of LLMs as common-
sense knowledge base for the robot to understand the context
of household tasks. They showed potential applicability of
LLMs but suggested that combined use of LLMs and other
knowledge sources would be effective. Lu et al. [12] devel-
oped a mechanism for extracting causal knowledge from the
database, which is similar to the current planning problem, to
include it within the next prompt. Ding et al. [13] developed
a system that can generate feasible and efﬁcient task plans
by symbolic and geometric inference by LLM followed
by feasibility checking. LLM-GROP enables the robot to
achieve tasks with underspeciﬁed requests using LLM while
guaranteeing feasibility using a separate checking module .
Sakim et al. [14] proposed to use a knowledge network [25]
for similar purpose.

B. Addressing Hallucination
LLMs sometimes suffer from hallucinations, which results
in generating incorrect outputs, or outputs inapplicable t o
the problem under consideration. In this work, the robot is
intended to work in ordinary house environments, and we
use an LLM to obtain information about object locations.
W e address the hallucination problem by (1) giving as a
prompt a set of concrete knowledge of the environment,
which is retreived from our ontological knowledge base, and
(2) verifying the consistency between LLM outputs with the
knowledge base. W e will describe these steps in detail in the
following sections.

A Human-in-the-loop Approach to Robot Action
Replanning through LLM Common-Sense Reasoning
Elena Merlo, Marta Lagomarsino, and Arash Ajoudani
Abstract—To facilitate the wider adoption of robotics, accessible
programming tools are required for non-experts. Observational
learning enables intuitive human skills transfer through hands-
on demonstrations, but relying solely on visual input can be
inefficient in terms of scalability and failure mitigation, especially
when based on a single demonstration. This paper presents a
human-in-the-loop method for enhancing the robot execution
plan, automatically generated based on a single RGB video,
with natural language input to a Large Language Model (LLM).
By including user-specified goals or critical task aspects and
exploiting the LLM common-sense reasoning, the system adjusts
the vision-based plan to prevent potential failures and adapts it
based on the received instructions. Experiments demonstrated
the framework intuitiveness and effectiveness in correcting
vision-derived errors and adapting plans without requiring
additional demonstrations. Moreover, interactive plan refinement
and hallucination corrections promoted system robustness.
Index Terms—Human-in-the-loop; Interactive planning; Large
Language Models (LLM); Failure mitigation
I. I NTRODUCTION
A
S robots become increasingly integrated into households
and workplaces [1], they have the potential to serve as
versatile tools for a wide range of tasks. This shift means that
more everyday users, with little to no experience in robotics
or programming, will need to instruct robots for their specific
needs. Programming by Demonstration (PbD) [2], [3] facilitates
the human skills transfer, allowing users to teach robots via
hands-on demonstrations rather than traditional coding. This
paradigm takes cues from human social learning processes
such as emulation [4].
Within PbD, observational learning [5] entails the systematic
observation of a human demonstrator and their surroundings to
identify relevant objects [6], monitor environmental changes,
and recognize actions along with their effects and preconditions
[7]. Through this process, the system can derive an execution
plan for the robot that preserves the logical sequence of steps
required to achieve a specific goal [8], [9] (e.g., placing a
pen in a case, cutting cheese, or cleaning a kitchen), and
replicate the demonstrated task by following analogous motion
patterns [10]. However, despite continuous advancements in
computer vision and action recognition [11], [12], human
and object motion tracking algorithms still make errors. This
makes visual information often insufficient for automatically
generating a reliable plan to replicate the execution, particularly
when relying on a single demonstration, as recommendable in
intuitive programming [13].
To address inaccuracies in visual data, researchers have
combined them with natural language descriptions, imple-

approaches are effective because they incorporate objects’
spatio-temporal relationships and trajectory-level data derived
from vision, while language clarifies any uncertain or missing
contextual information and specifies high-level intentions or
task goals that may not be apparent from the visual data
alone. This helps in performing successful robot replicas of
the learned task in different contexts, even when providing a
limited number of demonstrations.
The emergence of pre-trained Large Language Models
(LLMs) [20] has enhanced the potential of video-text integration
in PbD. Their extensive training enables flexible, template-
free language input and allows the generation of complex,
enriched outputs that go beyond the provided inputs by
leveraging the model internal knowledge. For instance, in [21],
[22], the user teaches skills to the robot by providing both
language descriptions and visual demonstrations to an LLM that
generates structured and generalizable manipulation programs,
incorporating high-level logic like loops and conditions. In [23]
the authors propose a multimodal pipeline that elaborates a
video demonstration integrating user language feedback to
generate task plans and extract key affordances for robot
execution. Despite these advances, LLMs can still hallucinate,
producing unfeasible or unsafe plans if not properly constrained
[24]. As an alternative, some works use LLMs not to generate
plans from scratch but to refine existing ones. In [25], [26],
the user provides corrective instructions, such as modifying
goals, adding constraints, or specifying waypoints, which the
LLM translates into adjustments to the robot’s motion plan. In
[27], real-time language corrections influence the selection and
adjustment of low-level actions by guiding high-level policy
decisions. While [28] proposes using LLMs to generate reward
functions, which are then optimized in real-time to bridge
high-level language instructions and low-level robot actions
for interactive task execution. In [29], the authors introduce
a shared autonomy system that maps high-level language
instructions and real-time verbal corrections into dynamic, low-
dimensional joystick control spaces, allowing users to guide
and refine a manipulator behavior during execution. Although
such approaches involve humans in the loop, the user’s role
is often limited to local corrections of robot motion during
execution, without visibility or control over the full task plan,
which they might want to review, modify, or personalize.
To address this challenge, we propose a pipeline that allows
users to interact with and refine a complete execution plan,
generated from a single RGB video demonstration of manual
tasks, through natural language. Building on our previous work,
the robot plan is generated as a Behavior Tree (BT) [8], as
shown in the gray block in Fig. 1, using Shannon’s Information
Theory (IT) to extract the task-relevant action sequence and
identify key hand trajectory waypoints. Since vision-based plans

II. M ETHODS
The pipeline consists of two modules as shown in Fig.
1. The first one (in gray) processes each frame of the
video demonstration, analyzing hand-object and object-object
interactions (HO and OO, respectively), using entropy measures
from Shannon’s IT. This analysis segments the executed task
into Interaction Units (IUs), temporal blocks where scene
interactions remain stable. By identifying the hand actions
that trigger IU transitions, a set of robot action primitives is
extracted and mapped into a BT plan. Note that semantics is
never used in this procedure; we study the information content
during interactions between video elements without assigning
meaning to them. For further details, refer to [8]. The second
module (main contribution of this paper, in yellow) enables
user’s interaction with the LLM to refine the generated BT and
execute it once satisfied. It consists of three sub-blocks: the
Plan Explainer, which translates the vision-based plan exeBT0
from numerical to semantic SemBT0, facilitating the user to
review and identify necessary refinements; the Plan Refiner,
which handles user’s requests and returns an updated plan
version using LLM; and the Plan Synthetizer, which reconverts
the final plan SemBTN into its executable version exeBTN for
the robot replica.

2) LLM-driven Plan Refiner: Once SemBT0 plan is gen-

modifications and improvements. The LLM is initially provided
with the SemBT0 in XML format, IPs labels of the involved
objects, structured refinement guidelines, and user requests.
Its task is to enhance the SemBT0 by correcting errors due
to vision limitations, such as perception inaccuracies, based
on user’s feedback. The guidelines provide the LLM with
a description of the XML structure and each node attribute.
Specifically, the three-label format for theExecTrajectory target-
posei attribute is highlighted, implicitly asking the model to
keep it. Moreover, for tasks it recognizes to involve contact
forces, it is instructed to include the stiffness attribute in
ExecTrajectory and specify the arm stiffness level: high for
precise tracking, medium (default) for general tasks, and low for
compliance-heavy tasks. The LLM-refined XML is checked for
structural correctness (e.g., node closures, tree integrity), and
missing elements are automatically fixed to ensure compatibility
with the execution pipeline. Then, SemBTj (with j = 1,..., N
denoting the refinement iteration) becomes available for user’s
review, allowing them to identify and address remaining logical
hallucinations or incoherent adaptations. Thus, once the errors
in the vision-based BT are addressed, the user can request
further adjustments to the plan to meet their current needs while
remaining in the loop. For instance, in the cup replacement task,
the user could specify that “the cup is full”. This additional
detail may lead the model to increase the transportation time of
the cup to reduce the probability of spilling. User’s commands
can also induce the LLM to remove or add some new nodes,
always keeping faith to the XML structural guidelines. The
LLM operates with contextual input, combining the guidelines
(static context) with dynamic context that includes the latest
user instruction and the most recent semantic plan SemBTj.
If the user is unsatisfied with the outcome, they can restore
the previous version SemBTj−1, effectively discarding the last
instruction and LLM output. This mechanism supports clearer
rephrasing and helps the system converge to a valid response.

III. E XPERIMENTS
The experimental campaign assessed the performance of
our method through three distinct experiments 1. First, we
conducted a pilot ablation study to demonstrate system func-
tionality by comparing robot performance with and without the
LLM-powered semantic abstraction layer. Secondly, a multi-
subject experiment assessed the robustness and usability of
the framework across users with varying levels of expertise in
robot programming. Finally, the LLM reasoning was tested with
high-level requests of varying complexity, and the resulting
strategies were analyzed.
A. Validation of Interactive Refinement of Vision-Based Plans
A pilot study was conducted in which a researcher interacted
with the model to refine failed vision-based robot plans
generated for two distinct human-demonstrated tasks: (i)
manipulating a jug to pour water into a glass and (ii) using a
sponge to clean the surface of a tray. The user engaged in the
interactive loop once per task. The difficulty of inferring the
correct jug orientation from visual data and the need to manage
interaction forces between the sponge and tray make these two
tasks challenging. We used a marker-based system (AruCo) to
detect the 6D poses of hands and objects during demonstrations,
attaching markers to the back of the hand and to objects to
preserve natural manipulation. Note that markerless object and
hand detection methods are rapidly advancing [30], offering
promising opportunities for integration into our framework. We
defined the following IPs for the two obkg, namely glass and
tray: IPglass = {left rim, right rim, center }, IPtray = {bottom-left
corner, bottom-right corner, top-left corner, top-right corner,
bottom-edge mid point, top-edge mid point, left-edge mid point,
right-edge mid point, center }. The jug was reduced to a single
point at its spout and the sponge to the central point of its
front edge, where we had attached the marker. To discriminate
the vertical relationship between om and obkg we set zth = 0.01
m. Instead, we chose zabove = 0.15 m and zbelow = −0.15 m
to ensure a clear separation between the objects and εz = 0
m to ensure their contact. The robot arm translation stiffness
values were set to 1000 N/m, 1500 N/m, and 2000 N/m
for low, medium, and high levels, respectively. Thanks to a
Graphical User Interface (GUI), the subject could check the
video demonstration and consult the automatically generated
plan translated into its semantic version (see the experimental
setup in Fig. 2), visualized using Groot2 software 1. Using
the microphone she could ask for some changes in the plan,
check again the updated plan and remain in the loop until the
plan was satisfactory enough to be sent for robot execution.
The LLM model we employed was GPT-4o from OpenAI [31],
while for converting audio requests to LLM prompts we used
Whisper, the automatic speech recognition model by OpenAI
[32]. Given the current pose of the objects, the exeBTN was
computed, and we compared the robot’s performance when
following the vision-based plan versus the LLM-refined plan.
B. Assessment of Framework Robustness and Usability
The multi-subject experiment assessed the framework’s
robustness and usability across different users. We asked 10
subjects (7 men and 3 women, with an average of 32.6 years) to
use our system to modify the vision-based plan of the cleaning
task2. Among the participants, subjects 4, 7, 8, and 9 had no
prior experience with robot programming. First, we proposed a
familiarization task to help users understand the request-to-BT
generation loop. Then, starting from the same BT, generated
using a “Z”-shaped cleaning motion on the top part of the
tray, participants were asked to refine it to clean the entire
surface. This was the only instruction; the refinement strategy
was up to them. At each iteration, users rated whether the
LLM plan modifications matched their requests by selecting:
Satisfied, Quite Satisfied, or Not Satisfied. Once completed the
task, participants evaluated their experience through the System
Usability Scale (SUS) questionnaire, rating 10 questions from
1 to 5. The experimenter also reviewed all request-BT pairs to
identify potential causes of users’ dissatisfaction. The average
number of refinement requests was also recorded.
C. Evaluation of LLM Reasoning in Plan Adaptation
In the third experiment, we tested LLM common-sense
reasoning by prompting it with 20 GPT-4o-generated high-
level requests to adjust a correct pouring plan, 10 to pour
less and 10 to pour more. We classified requests as medium
complexity if they included explicitly pour with less or more (or
synonyms), and high complexity if the command was implicit
(see Table I). The experimenter assessed whether each request
was fulfilled and identified the strategy used by the LLM.

V. D ISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION
We introduced a novel human-in-the-loop framework that
enables non-expert users to refine high-level robot plans,
generated from a single video demonstration, via natural
language interaction with an LLM. Unlike prior approaches
focused on low-level motion correction during execution,
our method supports iterative, pre-execution refinement of
interpretable BTs, granting users global control over the entire
task plan that they can correct and customize. This work
demonstrates that LLM reasoning at a semantic level helps
refine numerical data extracted from vision, such as trajectory
waypoints, while also suggesting necessary modifications to
non-visible parameters like arm stiffness. A current limitation
is that the supported BTs consist of basic action sequences and
lack condition checks (e.g., object presence) and parallelism,
which would enable multi-agent execution. They also rely
on a minimal set of action nodes that do not support more
complex manipulations such as pushing or pulling, which
would require dedicated nodes and parameters. Yet, future

Abstract—Large Language Models (LLMs) based robotic 
instruction generation and control methods provide flexible and 
efficient solutions for autonomous robot decision-making with 
natural language interaction and task generalization. However, 
traditional LLMs struggle to adapt to the dynamic physical 
environments and address the ambiguity of natural language 
instructions. Therefore, a mechanism that integrates physical 
environment information and guides users to clarify specific needs 
through human-computer interaction is required. To this end, we 
propose the SC-RAG-CoT framework, which combines spatially 
constrained retrieval augmented generation (SC-RAG) and Chain 
of Thought (CoT) interaction for robotic instruction generation. 
SC-RAG-CoT employs SC-RAG to retrieve contextual 
information from spatial knowledge bases (e.g., spatial data, object 
attributes) to mitigate hallucinations caused by environmental 
dynamics. Additionally, CoT decomposes ambiguous instructions 
into multi- step reasoning chains. T he reliability of instruction 
generation is improved through explicit verification of 
intermediate logic, and users are guided to gradually clarify their 
needs through interactive mechanisms. Simulation and ablation 
experiments demonstrate that the proposed SC-RAG-CoT 
strategy reduces average communication rounds by 2.6 and failure 
rates by 25.3% compared to traditional LLM-based methods in 
standardized test scenarios. 
Keywords—robot control; large language model; chain of 
thought; retrieval augmented generation; i nstruction 
disambiguation 
I. INTRODUCTION  
In recent years, generative artificial intelligence technologies 
represented by Large Language Models (LLMs) [1] provided 
new methodologies for robotic autonomous decision-making 
and task planning. These technologies demonstrate advantages 
in scenarios such as home services, industrial inspection, and 
healthcare, endowing robots with powerful task generalization 
and instruction adaptability, thereby substantially enhancing 
their intelligent capabilities. 
However, the inherent limitations of LLM-powered robots to 
perform human-serving tasks are increasingly apparent in 
dynamic physical environments. The disadvantages are 
primarily manifested in two aspects: 
(1) Dynamic Scenario Adaptation Challenge: LLMs rely on 
static parametric knowledge from pre-training contexts [2], 
making them ill-equipped to dynamic changes in physical 
environments. When objects are relocated, new obstacles 
emerge, or user requirements evolve, the lack of integration 
between LLMs and physical constraints (e.g., spatial 
accessibility, object properties) triggers "hallucinations" [3]. The 
hallucination prevents LLMs from adapting to real-world 
scenarios requiring robotic interaction. While fine-tuning [4] can 
update LLM knowledge, the depend on offline training and 
result in long update cycles and delayed responses to 
environmental changes. Furthermore, single-scenario fine-
tuning exhibits poor generalization, failing to accommodate 
complex and dynamic environmental demands. 
(2) Cognitive Deficiency in Physical Environments: As 
LLMs inherently process dimensionless data entities, they 
struggle to accurately interpret ambiguous human instructions, 
leading to imprecise robotic instruction generation [5]. For 
instance, the instruction "clean the table" may involve multiple 
potential actions (e.g., removing clutter, wiping surfaces, or 
reorganizing items). Without explicit contextual grounding, 
LLMs tend to generate task plans misaligned with user intent. 
To address these challenges, we propose SC-RAG-CoT, 
which integrates Spatially Constrained Retrieval-Augmented 
Generation (SC-RAG) and Chain of Thought (CoT)-guided 
human-robot interaction. SC-RAG-CoT aims to break through 
existing bottlenecks from two dimensions: environmental 
dynamics and ambiguous intent clarification. SC-RAG adopts 
physical constraints such as spatial coordinates objective 
physical laws into the generation process, to alleviate the 
"hallucinations" caused by the static knowledge of LLMs. CoT 
aims to resolve ambiguity in robot-human interaction by 
explicitly decomposing high-level intentions through multi-step 
reasoning chains, initiating interactive inquiries at critical nodes 
(e.g., target selection, operation determination). This transforms 
open-ended instructions into closed-loop decision sequences, 
achieving progressive alignment of "user intent-environment 
constraints-instruction generation."

II. RELATED WORK 
A. Retrieval-Augmented Generation 
Lewis et al. [6] pioneered the integration of pre-trained 
sequence models with Wikipedia dense vector indices and 
proposed a universal framework that fuses parametric and non-
parametric memory. Jiang et al. [7] developed the FLARE 
method, which triggers iterative retrieval by predicting 
confidence levels of future sentences. Yan et al. [8] introduced 
CRAG, incorporating a lightweight retrieval evaluator coupled 
with large-scale web search, enhancing system robustness 
through document decomposition and reorganization 
mechanisms. As to the spatial optimization, Yu et al. [9] 
constructed Spatial-RAG framework. Spatial-RAG balances 
spatial constraints and semantic associations in tourism 
geographic reasoning by combining spatial database retrieval 
with semantic similarity computation. Ding et al. [10] migrated 
RAG to autonomous driving simulation. The RealGen system 
achieves controllable generation of complex traffic scenarios 
through retrieval-based contextual learning, demonstrating 
cross-domain application potential. 
However, existing RAG variants predominantly rely on pre-
processed static knowledge bases. When applied to robotic 
physical environments, static knowledge bases struggle to adapt 
to dynamic changes. For example, if an apple is initially located 
in the living room at 𝑡𝑡1 but moved to the kitchen by a robot at 𝑡𝑡2. 
The outdated knowledge bases (unupdated post-operation) will 
cause scene understanding deviations during instruction 
execution. To address this, our SC-RAG introduces a dynamic 
knowledge base update mechanism based on historical 
operations, ensuring subsequent instructions reflect real-time 
environmental dynamics and effectively mitigating reasoning 
hallucinations caused by scene variability. 
B. A mbiguous  Instruction Disambiguation 
In ambiguous instruction disambiguation, Magassouba et al. 
[11] developed MMC-GAN for object placement semantic 
disambiguation tasks by fusing environmental context and 
robotic physical constraints. In addition, Weerakoon et al. [12] 
introduced gesture interaction modalities and constructed the 
M2Gestic system to improve instruction understanding accuracy 
by 30%. Niwa et al. [13] proposed the AmbignNLG, enhancing 
generation alignment through task ambiguity classification. Kim 
et al. [14] designed the APA pipeline to endow language models 
with explicit ambiguity perception and processing capabilities. 
Nevertheless, current disambiguation frameworks heavily 
depend on multimodal inputs (e.g., gestures, vision). There is 
limited research on quantifying ambiguity degrees in natural 
language instruction parsing. The Chain of Thought (CoT) 
proposed by Wei et al. [15] significantly enhanced LLM 
performance in mathematical reasoning and commonsense tasks 
through step-by-step reasoning exemplars. Building on this, our 
study integrates CoT theory with reasoning tree principles, 
defining joint ambiguity as the criterion for initiating interactive 
guidance.

table" without specifying the target table location or cleaning 
method) or containing semantic ambiguity (e.g., "get the drink" 
may imply "fetch" or "hold"). Such instructions carry risks of 
misalignment between the LLM-generated action sequences and 
the user's true intent due to missing critical parameters or strong 
contextual dependencies. Specifically, the Joint Ambiguity 
Degree is composed of two components:   
(1) Spatial Ambiguity: Unclear target object locations or 
motion paths (e.g., "put the book back" without specifying the 
shelf position).   
(2) Semantic Ambiguity: Ambiguous action types or object 
attributes (e.g., "pick up the fruit" involving multiple fruit types).

IV. EXPERIMENTS 
A. Experimental Settings 
The experimental server configuration includes a 16-core 
Xeon(R) Platinum 8352V CPU and 2 Nvidia GeForce RTX 
4090D GPUs (24GB), with GPU driver version 560.35.03 and 
CUDA 12.6. The LLM in the experiments is LLaMA2-7B. The 
model dimension is 4096 and it supports a maximum input 
length of 2048 tokens.   
Our benchmark test environment, following [3], simulates a 
four-room household setup in Gazebo. The scenario includes:  
Living rooms (×2), Bedrooms (×4), Kitchen (×1), Bathrooms 
(×3) with 30 types of objects (e.g., apples, tables, chairs) totaling 
112 items. Three task categories are defined: retrieve {item}, 
clean {location}, and search for {item}. All objects store their 
geometric spatial features (coordinates, access paths, geometric 
regions), textual attribute descriptions, and interaction history in 
a local dynamic library.  The evaluation metrics include:  rounds 
of dialogue to generate the final executable instruction and the 
failure rate of instruction execution.

V. CONCLUSION 
In this paper, we propose the SC-RAG-CoT framework, 
which integrates Spatial-Contextual Retrieval-Augmented 
Generation (SC-RAG) and Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning, 
offering an innovative solution to address the challenges of 
LLMs in generating instructions for dynamic environments. By 
leveraging SC-RAG to dynamically fuse spatial constraints and 
physical attributes from the scenario knowledge base, the 
framework explicitly suppresses the generation of instructions 
conflicting with the dynamic environment. CoT decomposes 
ambiguous instructions into interactively verifiable logical 
chains, guiding users to clarify requirements at critical decision 
nodes. Experimental results demonstrate that SC-RAG-CoT 
reduces the communication round of human-robot interaction 
and effectively mitigates instruction execution failure risks. 
Future research will explore mechanisms for deep integration of 
multimodal sensory data, optimize the update efficiency of 
dynamic knowledge bases through incremental learning, and 
further enhance the semantic-spatial mapping capability for 
complex unstructured instructions, thereby extending the 
framework’s robustness and applicability in open-world 
environments.
